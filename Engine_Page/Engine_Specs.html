250 Inline 6

Displacement: 250 cu in (4.1L) Horsepower: ~155 hp Torque: 235 lb-ft C10 Years: 1967–1972

The 250 inline-six engine, also known as the 4.1L, was part of Chevrolet’s “High Torque” series and was produced from 1966 to 1984. It featured a single one-barrel carburetor and delivered around 155 horsepower at 4200 RPM with 235 lb-ft of torque at 1600 RPM. Known for its smooth operation and solid reliability, the 250 was a popular choice in Chevy and GMC trucks, offering a good balance of power and fuel efficiency for its time.


283 Small Block V8

Displacement: 283 cu in (4.6L) Horsepower: ~195 hp Torque: ~285 lb-ft C10 / GMC Years: 1967 only

The 283 cubic-inch (4.6L) small-block V8 first showed up back in 1957 and stayed in production through 1967. By the time the new body-style trucks arrived that year, the 283 was nearing the end of its run, making it one of only two engines offered exclusively in 1967 Chevy and GMC trucks. Despite its smaller size, the 283 delivered strong, dependable performance and helped establish the reputation of the small-block Chevy family.


305 60-Degree V6

Displacement: 305 cu in (5.0L) Horsepower: ~165 hp Torque: ~280 lb-ft GMC ½-ton Years: 1967–1969

The 305 cubic-inch (5.0L) V6 was a GMC-exclusive engine known for its rugged, heavy-duty construction and tremendous low-end torque. Unlike Chevrolet’s lighter V8s, the 305 V6 used a cast-iron block and heads designed to handle demanding workloads. Rated around 165 horsepower and 280 lb-ft of torque, the 305 was ideal for towing and hauling, earning a long-standing reputation for durability in GMC’s classic truck lineup.


307 Small Block V8

Displacement: 307 cu in (5.0L) Horsepower: ~200 hp Torque: ~300 lb-ft C10 / GMC Years: 1968–1972

The 307 cubic-inch (5.0L) small-block V8 was introduced in 1968 as the successor to the 283. Using the 283’s bore combined with the 327’s stroke, the 307 delivered smooth performance and practical reliability. Producing around 200 horsepower and 300 lb-ft of torque, it offered a balanced mix of power and efficiency. The 307 became known as a dependable workhorse throughout the late 1960s and early 1970s.


327 Small Block V8

Displacement: 327 cu in (5.4L) Horsepower: ~210 hp Torque: ~320 lb-ft C10 / GMC Years: 1967–1969

The 327 cubic-inch (5.4L) small-block V8 debuted in the early 1960s and quickly became one of Chevrolet’s most respected engines. Using a 4.00-inch bore and a short 3.25-inch stroke, the 327 was designed for quick revving and strong performance across the RPM range. Depending on the version, output ranged from roughly 210 horsepower to more than 300 horsepower. The 327 became well-known for its smooth, responsive power delivery and classic small-block durability.


350 Small Block V8

Displacement: 350 cu in (5.7L) Horsepower: ~255 hp Torque: ~350 lb-ft C10 / GMC Years: 1969–1972

The 350 cubic-inch (5.7L) small-block V8 debuted in the late 1960s and quickly became one of Chevrolet’s most iconic and widely used engines. Featuring a 4.00-inch bore and 3.48-inch stroke, the 350 delivered smooth, consistent torque and dependable power suitable for work or everyday driving. Depending on the configuration, output ranged from around 250 horsepower to well over 300 horsepower in high-performance versions. The 350 earned a reputation for versatility, durability, and the strong performance that helped define GM’s small-block legacy.


351 V6 (GMC)

Displacement: 351 cu in (5.8L) Horsepower: ~175 hp Torque: ~300 lb-ft GMC Years: 1967–1972

The 351 cubic-inch (5.8L) GMC V6 was the larger successor to the earlier 305 V6, engineered for heavier-duty workloads and improved torque output. Built with the same rugged cast-iron construction GMC was known for, the 351 delivered excellent low-RPM pulling power and dependable operation under load. With output ranging from roughly 175 to nearly 200 horsepower and torque ratings exceeding 300 lb-ft, the 351 V6 became a popular choice in GMC ¾-ton and 1-ton trucks throughout the late 1960s and early 1970s. Its durability and unique V6 design earned it a long-standing reputation among classic GMC owners.


396 Big Block V8

Displacement: 396 cu in (6.5L) Horsepower: ~325 hp Torque: ~410 lb-ft C10 / C20 Years: 1968–1972

The 396 cubic-inch (6.5L) big-block V8 delivered serious power and torque for Chevrolet’s heavy-duty truck lineup. Featuring a large-bore, long-stroke design and high-flow cylinder heads, the 396 offered impressive low-end strength and confident acceleration even with substantial loads. Factory ratings varied from around 325 to 375 horsepower, with torque figures exceeding 400 lb-ft. Known for its strong performance and unmistakable big-block character, the 396 provided the kind of capability needed for towing, hauling, and demanding work environments.


402 Big Block V8

Displacement: 402 cu in (6.6L) Horsepower: ~240 hp Torque: ~400 lb-ft C10 / C20 Years: 1970–1972

The 402 cubic-inch (6.6L) big-block V8 was introduced in 1970 as the replacement for the 396, although Chevrolet continued to badge it as a “400” in many trucks. Built with a slightly larger bore, the 402 produced strong torque and smooth power delivery, making it a popular choice for ¾-ton and 1-ton applications. Rated between roughly 240 and 300 horsepower depending on configuration, the 402 offered dependable big-block performance with the durability needed for heavy hauling and work use. Its combination of reliability, torque, and unmistakable big-block sound made it a favorite among classic Chevy truck owners.
